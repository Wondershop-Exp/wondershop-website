Leaked: The party planner's checklist we use for every birthday

📋 Planning Guide · 5 min read · By Shruti Garg, Wondershop
⭐ 8 steps · Used across 500+ parties in Mumbai ⭐
← Back to Blog

Every time we sit down to plan a birthday at Wondershop, we go through the same 8-step checklist. It doesn't matter if the party is for 15 kids at home or 80 guests at a banquet hall — the process is always the same.

We're sharing it here exactly as we use it. No fluff, no filler. Just the actual things a professional party planner thinks through before every celebration.

1
Step One
The Interview — ask the birthday star first

Before any planning happens, we sit down with the most important person in the room: the birthday child. Not the parents. The child.

We ask them two things:

  • What theme do you love right now? Unicorns, dinosaurs, superheroes, football, K-pop — no rules, everything is valid.
  • How many friends do you want there?

Kids are usually very clear about what they want. The theme they name at age 6 or 8 or 10 is their current world — and a birthday that reflects their world is the one they remember for years.

💡 Parents often want to "surprise" the child with a theme they think the child will love. That's sweet, but risky. Ask the child. You'll get a clearer answer, and the child will feel genuinely celebrated rather than managed.
2
Step Two
Plan the Space — before anything else is booked

Once you know the theme and rough guest count, spend 20 minutes thinking through the physical space. Not decorating it — just sketching how it'll work.

Key zones to map out:

  • Décor area — where's the main backdrop? Where does the feature wall go?
  • Cake table — it needs to be visible, well-lit, and accessible for the cake-cutting moment
  • Welcome board — at the entrance, so it's the first thing guests see
  • Activity zone — enough room for kids to move, not bumping into the food table
  • Seating and catering tables — for adults who want to sit, and for food service

A simple floor plan — even a rough sketch on your phone — makes setup day dramatically smoother. It also means your decor team arrives with a clear brief rather than figuring things out under time pressure.

And don't forget: plan for waste management. More guests means more mess. Keep someone on standby for garbage disposal and quick mid-party cleanups. This is the detail nobody thinks about until there's a pile of paper cups near the cake table.

💡 Create a rough party timeline too — when guests arrive, when activities start, when the cake is cut, when food is served, and when it winds down. A party without a timeline often runs 45 minutes long and ends chaotically.
3
Step Three
Host & Music — the invisible backbone of a great party

A great host and the right music can completely change the energy of a party — and take an enormous amount of pressure off you as the parent.

The host keeps the narrative going, manages the activity flow, handles the kids during transitions, and makes sure nobody is standing around looking lost. Without a host, that job falls on you. And you have enough to do.

For music, don't outsource this to a generic playlist. Create a playlist with the birthday child's favourite songs — the ones they know the words to, the ones they dance to at home. When kids hear their favourite song at their own party, something magical happens.

💡 Keep small prizes ready for game winners — stickers, small toys, or chocolates. The host will need them throughout the party. These don't need to be expensive; the thrill of winning something is what matters.
4
Step Four
Engagement Plan — a room full of excited kids needs things to do

This is the step most DIY parties skip — and it's the reason many parties feel flat despite beautiful décor. Décor makes a party look good. Activities make it feel alive.

Plan a mix of engagement setups that work simultaneously so kids can drift between them naturally:

  • DIY crafts — keychain-making, bracelet stations, tote bag painting
  • Tattoo stations — theme-matched temporary tattoos are universally loved
  • Face painting — a classic that never fails, especially for ages 3–9
  • Art corners — colouring sheets, sticker art, or mini canvases
  • Food activity stations — popcorn bars, candy stations, or cookie decorating

Interactive setups keep kids engaged, prevent the "what do we do now?" moments, and give parents a few minutes to actually talk to each other.

💡 Don't plan activities that require all children to stop and participate at once — it's impossible to herd 20 excited 7-year-olds into the same place at the same time. Stations they can visit freely work far better.
5
Step Five
Décor — get specific before setup day

Vague decor briefs lead to last-minute surprises, and last-minute surprises on the morning of a party are not fun. The more specific you can be in advance, the smoother setup day goes.

When you sit down with your event planner (or your decor team), come prepared with:

  • Reference images — Pinterest boards, Instagram saves, or screenshots from parties you've loved
  • Balloon colour palette — specific shades matter more than you think
  • Backdrop style — single panel, 3-panel, balloon arch, fabric backdrop?
  • Décor zones — which areas need décor? Entrance, main wall, cake table, welcome board?
  • Cake table cover — colour, style, whether you want a skirt or simple cloth
  • Venue handover timings — when can setup begin, and when must everything be done?
💡 Visualising the full setup in advance — even a rough mental walkthrough — helps catch conflicts early. "Where does the welcome board go if the entrance is only 3 feet wide?" is a much better question to answer on a planning call than at 8am on party day.
6
Step Six
Cake & Food — keep it simple, go with trusted vendors

A birthday party with average food and a show-stopping cake will be remembered as a great party. A party with great food and a disappointing cake will not. The cake is the centrepiece — plan it accordingly.

For the cake:

  • Book your baker at least 3–4 weeks in advance for a custom design
  • Share a clear reference image — don't describe it, show it
  • Plan the cake table setup in advance: height, surrounding décor, lighting if possible
  • Confirm delivery timing and who is responsible for transport

For food, the rule is: keep it simple and go with trusted vendors. A party is not the day to experiment with a new caterer. Stick with people you've used before or that come with strong recommendations. Kids are not adventurous eaters at parties — they want familiar, finger-food-friendly food they can grab while running between activity stations.

💡 Plan the cake-cutting moment into your timeline. It tends to get pushed later and later as the party runs long. Set a fixed time and tell your host — they'll make sure it happens.
7
Step Seven
Piñata — always a highlight, never an afterthought

The piñata moment is the most kinetic, most joyful, most chaotic moment in any kids' party. Plan it properly and it becomes a memory. Skip the planning and it becomes a scramble.

Three things to sort in advance:

  • Shape — match it to the theme. A star, a character, an animal — whatever fits the world you've built. Custom piñata shapes can be ordered 2 weeks in advance.
  • Fillings — chocolates, small toys, stickers, temporary tattoos. Mix edibles with small prizes so it's not just a sugar drop.
  • Bags for collecting — this is the detail that gets missed. Without bags, kids run around with handfuls of candy that fall, get stepped on, and cause tears. A small kraft paper bag or a themed pouch for each child, handed out before the piñata breaks, makes the whole thing civilised and fun.
💡 Position the piñata moment strategically in your timeline — typically towards the end of the activity portion, just before cake. It creates a natural energy peak that flows perfectly into the cake-cutting celebration.
8
Step Eight
Return Gifts — organised, thoughtful, not an afterthought

Return gifts are the last thing guests take home — and therefore the last impression your party makes. A well-chosen, well-presented return gift extends the memory of the party by another day or two.

Two things to get right:

  • Set up a dedicated return gift table — a designated spot that's clearly separate from food and activity areas, visible near the exit. It sounds like a small detail. It makes gift distribution at the end of a party dramatically more organised than chasing parents with bags as they're leaving.
  • Choose gifts kids will actually use — themed gifts are fun, but a gift that gets used every day (a notebook, a water bottle, a small kit) means your party is remembered every time they use it. Gifts that end up in a drawer a week later don't serve anyone.
💡 Label return gift bags with each child's name if you have the guest list. It prevents the scramble at the end and gives parents something to hand their child specifically — "this one's for you" lands very differently than "grab one from the table."
